A letter from the founders — Spring 2026

Something changed when we started working together. Not just the work itself — though that changed too — but our relationship to the tools, the process, the whole idea of what software can feel like when it's built with intention.

We began this studio with a simple conviction: that most digital work is done too fast, under too little consideration, and delivered without the care it deserves. We had seen the results. Projects launched and immediately abandoned. Automation built to impress rather than to function. AI integrations that created more confusion than they resolved.

What we offer instead is a slower, more deliberate kind of practice. We design and build web presences that reflect the genuine character of the businesses behind them — not templates dressed up, but actual thinking made visible. We build automation that earns its place, reducing friction where friction doesn't belong. And we work with AI not as a novelty, but as a craft material: something to be shaped, tested, and trusted only after it proves itself.

This issue — our first, and we hope the beginning of a longer conversation — contains examples of that work, reflections on process, and an open invitation to anyone who believes their digital infrastructure deserves the same attention they give to everything else they care about.

We are grateful you found your way here.

— Alex × Nikolay, Founders

Feature 01

Web Development

We build websites the way a furniture maker builds a table: with attention to how it will be used, who will use it, and whether it will still hold its shape in ten years. This means starting from structure before aesthetics, thinking in systems before components, and writing code that a future developer can read without needing to ask questions.

Our web work ranges from editorial platforms to e-commerce to bespoke business applications. The common thread is that every project begins with a conversation about what the work is actually for — and ends only when we are satisfied the answer is visible in the result.

Next.js — React — TypeScript — Tailwind
Feature 02

Automation Pipelines

Most businesses carry invisible weight — manual processes that consume hours every week, data that lives in three different systems and has to be reconciled by hand, reports that require a dedicated person to produce. Automation work is, at its core, the practice of finding that weight and removing it.

We map workflows before we build anything. We question whether automation is the right solution, or whether the process itself needs redesign first. When we do build, we build things that run quietly, notify you when something needs attention, and are comprehensible enough to be maintained by someone who wasn't in the room when they were created.

Feature 03

AI Agents

We use the word agent carefully. An agent is not a chatbot with a prompt. It is a system that reasons, plans, and acts — calling tools, managing context, deciding what to do next without someone telling it at every step. Building a good one requires understanding what it is trying to accomplish, what information it needs to do that, and where the boundaries of its authority should be.

We build agents for specific, well-defined tasks: research and synthesis, document processing, customer interaction routing, data extraction from unstructured sources. We do not build agents that are meant to do everything — we build agents that do one thing well enough to trust.

Claude API — OpenAI — LangGraph — MCP

Project 01 — Full Spread

VIP Rental Phuket

Fig. 04 — VIP Rental Phuket, property portal, 2025 — Art direction and full-stack development

A premium property rental platform for a Phuket-based real estate company operating at the high end of the short-term rental market. The brief was specific: create something that felt as considered as the properties themselves — and that would not embarrass the agent when shown to a client at the level they were accustomed to dealing with.

We designed and built the platform from scratch: property listings with high-resolution photography management, availability calendars integrated with the booking backend, an enquiry system that routes and qualifies leads automatically, and a CMS that the team could operate without technical assistance. The result is used daily and has not required a developer call since launch.

How We Work Pages 40 – 48
Method

How We Work — A Process Essay

01

Listen Before Everything

The first conversations are not about technology. They are about the business: how it runs today, where it strains, what would need to be true for the next year to look different from the last. We ask questions that might seem beside the point — who uses this system, at what time of day, what happens when it fails — because context is the material that makes good decisions possible.

We treat the listening phase as a deliverable, not a formality. At the end of it, both parties should have a shared document describing the problem in enough detail that the solution can be evaluated against it.

"A specification written before the listening is done is not a specification — it is a guess dressed in the language of certainty."

02

Design the System, Not the Screen

Most project failures are architecture failures, not implementation failures. Before any code is written, we produce a model of the system: what are its components, how do they communicate, where does the data live, what happens when something breaks. Visual design follows from this — not the other way around.

This does not mean the work is slow. It means the work does not need to be redone. There is an economy to deliberate design that only becomes visible over the lifetime of a project.

03

Build and Transfer

We build in stages, showing work early and adjusting on evidence rather than assumption. Every decision made during the build is documented — not in exhaustive technical prose, but in clear language that the person responsible for the system after us can understand without a glossary.

Handover is not an afterthought. The transfer of a working system that no one can maintain is a partial failure. We consider our work complete only when the people who will operate what we built feel confident doing so.

04

Stay Close After Launch

The weeks immediately following launch are where most of what was learned during a project becomes available for use. We remain engaged during this period — available to observe, adjust, and answer questions — because real use surfaces things that tests do not.

Our preference is for relationships that extend beyond the initial engagement. We are better collaborators with clients we understand, and we understand them better with each successive project. This is not a commercial calculation — it is a statement about how good work actually gets made.
